Major Car Systems Explained

Cars are made of interconnected systems. Here's how the most important ones work in plain English.

üî•

Engine System (Combustion)

How it works:

  1. Fuel and air mix inside cylinders.
  2. A spark plug ignites the mixture, creating a small explosion.
  3. The explosion pushes a piston down.
  4. The piston turns a crankshaft, which eventually turns the wheels.

In short: Tiny controlled explosions create the power to move your car.

Braking System

How it works:

  1. You press the brake pedal.
  2. Brake fluid flows through lines to each wheel.
  3. The fluid pushes brake pads against spinning rotors (discs).
  4. Friction slows the rotation, which slows the car.

In short: Friction converts your car's motion into heat, bringing you to a stop.

Transmission System

How it works:

  1. The engine spins at thousands of RPM (revolutions per minute).
  2. The transmission uses gears to adjust that speed for the wheels.
  3. Low gears = more power, less speed (for starting/hills).
  4. High gears = less power, more speed (for cruising).

In short: Gears let the engine run efficiently at any speed.

Cooling System

How it works:

  1. Coolant (liquid) flows through passages in the engine block.
  2. It absorbs heat from combustion.
  3. The hot coolant flows to the radiator.
  4. Air passing through the radiator cools the liquid down.
  5. The cooled liquid returns to the engine. Repeat.

In short: Coolant circulates to keep the engine from melting itself.

Electrical System

How it works:

  1. The battery provides electricity to start the engine.
  2. Once running, the alternator generates electricity.
  3. Electricity powers lights, radio, windows, sensors, and the computer (ECU).
  4. The alternator also recharges the battery.

In short: A loop of generation and storage keeps all electronics running.

Steering System

How it works:

  1. You turn the steering wheel.
  2. A shaft connects the wheel to a gear mechanism.
  3. The mechanism turns the front wheels left or right.
  4. Power steering (hydraulic or electric) makes turning easier.

In short: Your input is mechanically amplified to turn heavy wheels with ease.

Suspension System

How it works:

  1. Springs and shock absorbers connect the wheels to the car body.
  2. When you hit a bump, springs compress to absorb the impact.
  3. Shocks control how fast springs bounce back.
  4. This keeps the tires on the road and the ride smooth.

In short: Springs and shocks smooth out bumps and keep you in control.

Fuel System

How it works:

  1. Fuel sits in a tank, usually near the rear axle.
  2. A pump pushes fuel through lines toward the engine.
  3. Fuel injectors spray precise amounts into each cylinder.
  4. The engine computer controls timing and quantity.

In short: Fuel is pumped and precisely metered to keep the engine running efficiently.

HVAC System (Climate Control)

How it works:

  1. The A/C compressor (powered by the engine) pressurizes refrigerant gas.
  2. The gas cools down in the condenser and becomes liquid.
  3. Cold liquid flows to the evaporator inside the cabin.
  4. A blower fan pushes air over the cold evaporator, cooling the cabin.
  5. Heat mode uses engine heat instead of refrigerant to warm the air.

In short: Refrigerant cycles between hot and cold to control cabin temperature.
